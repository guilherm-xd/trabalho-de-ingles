CORE MESSAGE

What The Song Says

Borders is a song about migration, identity and inequality. It questions why some people can move freely around the world while others face barriers, discrimination and exclusion - simply because they were born in the wrong place.

Instead of presenting abstract political arguments, M.I.A. uses simple and direct language. By listing the systems of control and repeating the same question after each one, she transforms frustration into artistic form.

SONG STRUCTURE

How The Song Is Built

The structure of Borders is intentionally repetitive. M.I.A. names a concept or system - borders, politics, identities, soldiers, papers, passports, uniforms - and then asks the same colloquial question after each one of them.

This repetition creates a cumulative effect: each named system adds weight to the critique. The simplicity of the question contrasts with the gravity of the topics, making the critique even more impactful.

The song also references life vests and crowded boats - images that directly evoke the dangerous crossings of refugees through the Mediterranean Sea, connecting the abstract to the concrete.

THEME-BY-THEME ANALYSIS

The Major Themes Of The Lyrics

THEME 01

The Central Question

The phrase "What's up with that?" is repeated throughout the entire song. It is a casual, colloquial American English expression - normally used in everyday situations - that M.I.A. turns into a political challenge. By using simple language for serious topics, she makes the critique both more accessible and more urgent.

"What's up with that?" - The simple question carries the full weight of the song's social critique.

THEME 02

Systems of Control

M.I.A. lists the mechanisms that control human movement: physical borders, documents (papers, passports), military presence (soldiers), political bureaucracy and socially assigned identities. Each listed item represents a layer of the system that decides who gets to move - and who does not.

🇧🇷 TRADUÇÃO / SIGNIFICADO

Borders, politics, identities, soldiers, papers, passports, uniforms, etiquettes - all these systems determine who has freedom of movement.

THEME 03

The Reality of Refugees

At the heart of the song, M.I.A. uses concrete images: life vests and overcrowded boats. These references directly evoke the 2015 Mediterranean crisis, when hundreds of thousands of refugees risked their lives to cross the sea. The song makes visible what borders hide.

🇧🇷 TRADUÇÃO / SIGNIFICADO

Life vests and overcrowded boats represent the human cost of borders - the danger refugees face when seeking safety.

THEME 04

Privilege and Freedom

Borders questions the fundamental inequality of the global system: holders of certain passports cross borders without difficulty, while holders of others risk their lives. The song criticizes the idea that the place of birth determines the value of a human life and access to opportunities.

🇧🇷 TRADUÇÃO / SIGNIFICADO

Some passports open doors; others close them. Where you are born determines your freedom - and M.I.A. questions why this is accepted.

THEME 05

Identity and Belonging

The song also explores who is considered to "belong" to a nation or society. Identity, in this context, is not just something personal - it is an externally imposed label that can determine whether you are accepted or rejected, included or excluded.

🇧🇷 TRADUÇÃO / SIGNIFICADO

Identities - what's up with that? - Identity ceases to be a right and becomes a selection criterion that decides who deserves protection.

THEME 06

The Music Video As Extension

The music video does not tell an individual story - it shows groups of people climbing fences, crossing barriers and sailing together in overcrowded boats. The emphasis on the collective, not the individual, is a political choice: forced displacement is not an isolated experience, but a phenomenon that affects millions.

🇧🇷 TRADUÇÃO / SIGNIFICADO

The choice to show crowds, not individuals, emphasizes that forced migration is a global phenomenon - not a single person's problem.

"What's up with that?"

The phrase that defines the song. Colloquial, direct and repeated - each time M.I.A. says it, another system of control is called into question. It is an invitation for the listener to stop and ask: why do we accept this?

LITERARY TECHNIQUE

The Power Of Repeated Questions

One of the most distinctive features of the song is the strategic use of repeated questions. Instead of providing answers or solutions, M.I.A. uses the question as a rhetorical tool - forcing the listener to confront each named topic.

This technique, called anaphora (the repetition of the same structure at the beginning of successive lines), creates a growing rhythm of indignation. The song does not explain - it questions. And by questioning repeatedly, it makes it impossible to ignore.

SYMBOLISM

What Borders Represent

In the song, borders represent far more than physical lines between countries. They symbolize social divisions, economic inequality, barriers to access and the logic of exclusion that defines who belongs and who does not.

The concept of borders becomes a metaphor for all the obstacles - visible and invisible - that prevent people from achieving safety, dignity and equal treatment. They are not just fences: they are laws, bureaucracy, prejudice and power.

Freedom

The ability to move, travel and seek opportunities - distributed unequally across the world.

Identity

Questions about belonging, acceptance and who has the right to exist in a given space.

Power

Who decides where people can go, live and seek safety - and based on what criteria.

Human Rights

The universal right to safety, dignity and opportunity - which borders frequently deny.

RELEVANCE

Why The Lyrics Still Resonate

The lyrics remain relevant because forced migration is still one of the most urgent global issues. In 2025, according to UNHCR (the UN Refugee Agency), over 41.6 million people worldwide had refugee status - a scale that makes M.I.A.'s questions even more necessary.

Borders reminds us that behind every statistic there is a human story filled with challenges, hopes and dreams. The song asks us not to forget that.

Borders asks questions that many societies still struggle to answer.

The song does not need to provide answers to be powerful. Its strength lies precisely in forcing us to ask: what is wrong with the world we have built?

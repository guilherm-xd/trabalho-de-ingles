REFUGEES

Why People Leave Their Homes

Refugees are people forced to leave their countries because of war, persecution, violence or serious threats to their safety. The fundamental difference between a refugee and a voluntary migrant is the absence of a real choice.

In 2015 - the year Borders was released - over one million people crossed the Mediterranean Sea toward Europe, most fleeing conflict in Syria, Afghanistan and Iraq. It was the largest movement of refugees seen in Europe since World War II.

Many refugees travel long distances under extremely dangerous conditions, paying people smugglers who exploit their desperation. Their journeys are often filled with unpredictable dangers.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT

The 2015 Refugee Crisis

Borders was released in November 2015 - the exact moment when the world was watching, in shock, the largest refugee flow in decades. Images of overcrowded boats in the Mediterranean, of desperate families at European borders and of Syrian toddler Alan Kurdi drowned on a Turkish beach dominated global news.

The Syrian civil war, which began in 2011, had displaced over 11 million people by 2015. Beyond Syrians, Afghans, Iraqis, Eritreans and Sudanese also made up the large flow of people seeking safety in Europe.

M.I.A. created Borders in direct response to these images and stories - using her position as an artist to amplify voices that were rarely heard.

IMMIGRATION

Challenges Faced By Migrants

Immigrants and refugees frequently face multiple layers of difficulty: language barriers, cultural differences, economic hardship and discrimination. In many cases, even after arriving in a destination country, they continue to be treated as "outsiders" for years or decades.

In many countries, debates about immigration have become highly polarized. Far-right political parties gained ground across Europe and the Americas promising to "control borders" - frequently dehumanizing migrants in political discourse.

Borders highlights the importance of seeing migrants as human beings with stories, dignity and rights - not as statistics or political threats.

THE MUSIC VIDEO

A Powerful Visual Message

The music video for Borders is one of the most memorable aspects of the project. Instead of telling the story of a single individual, it shows large groups of people - climbing fences, crossing barriers and sailing together in crowded boats.

The aesthetic choice to show crowds rather than individuals is intentional: it emphasizes that forced displacement is a collective and global phenomenon, not a problem of isolated people who "made wrong choices."

The scenes directly represent the reality faced by refugees: barbed wire fences, boats on the verge of sinking, exhausted bodies arriving on shore. M.I.A. transforms these images into art so that audiences cannot ignore them.

TODAY

Connection With The Current World

The themes explored in Borders remain highly relevant. In 2025, Sudan remained the world's largest internal displacement crisis, with 9.1 million people displaced within the country. Venezuela produced one of the largest refugee crises in Latin American history.

Ongoing conflicts, economic crises, environmental disasters and climate change continue to force millions of people to leave their homes - often without choice and without any guarantee of safety at the destination.

Governments around the world still debate migration policies, border security and refugee protection - often prioritizing national security over human rights. This is why M.I.A.'s questions remain urgent.

Migration

A global phenomenon affecting every continent - over 70% of refugees live in countries neighboring their home country.

Human Rights

The 1951 Refugee Convention guarantees the right to asylum. In 2026, the world marks the 75th anniversary of this foundational treaty.

Identity

Over 4.5 million people worldwide are stateless - without nationality, without a passport and without access to basic rights.

Equality

68% of refugees are hosted by low- and middle-income countries - not by the world's wealthiest nations, as is often claimed.

CONCLUSION

The Message Of Borders

Borders is more than a song about migration. It is a reflection on inequality, identity, freedom and human dignity - anchored in real facts and in a crisis that has not ended.

Through both music and visual storytelling, M.I.A. encourages audiences to question assumptions about borders and to recognize the humanity behind every migration story.

The song reminds us that discussions about refugees and immigrants are ultimately discussions about people, rights and what kind of world we want to build.

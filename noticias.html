GLOBAL REPORT
June 2026 · UNHCR Global Trends Report

Global displacement falls for the first time in a decade - but still affects 117 million

The annual UNHCR report, published in June 2026, revealed that the total number of forcibly displaced people fell 4% in 2025, reaching 117.8 million. It was the first recorded decrease in a decade. However, 41.6 million still held refugee status - a number the UN High Commissioner called "unacceptably high." Over 70% of refugees remain in prolonged exile, many living below the poverty line.

M.I.A. asked in 2015: "what's up with that?" In 2026, the question still has no satisfactory answer: 117 million reasons to keep questioning.

HUMANITARIAN CRISIS
2024–2025 · Mediterranean Sea

Over 2,300 deaths in the Mediterranean in 2024: the world's most dangerous route remains active

According to the International Organization for Migration (IOM), over 2,300 people died or disappeared trying to cross the Mediterranean Sea in 2024 - an 18% increase from 2023. Around 208,000 people arrived irregularly in Europe via the Mediterranean that year. In 2025, 1,745 deaths have already been recorded. The central route, from North Africa to Italy, was the deadliest, with 1 in every 120 people who attempted the crossing dying in the process.

CONNECTION TO BORDERS

The life vests and overcrowded boats in M.I.A.'s lyrics are not metaphors: they are the documented reality of 2,300 people who died trying to reach safety.

WORLD'S LARGEST CRISIS
2024–2025 · Sudan

Sudan: the planet's largest internal displacement crisis, with 9.1 million people

The conflict that began in Sudan in April 2023 generated the world's largest internal displacement crisis. By the end of 2025, 9.1 million Sudanese were displaced within the country, and another 2.8 million became refugees in neighboring countries. The conflict, which entered its fourth year in 2025, destroyed basic infrastructure and left millions without access to essential services, housing or livelihoods.

CONNECTION TO BORDERS

Sudan shows that borders do not protect people - they trap them. 9.1 million need to cross borders to survive, but often cannot.

IMMIGRATION POLICY
2025 · Iran and Pakistan

2.9 million Afghans return under pressure: when "going home" is not a free choice

In 2025, Iran and Pakistan drastically tightened their policies toward Afghan refugees - leading to the return of 2.9 million Afghans, including 1.9 million recognized refugees. The number was five times higher than the previous year. According to UNHCR, many of these returns were not voluntary: the Afghans reported they simply had no other option. Many returned to a country in economic collapse, devastated by decades of conflict.

CONNECTION TO BORDERS

M.I.A. questions passports and papers - the very documents that determined that 2.9 million Afghans had no right to remain where they were.

HUMAN RIGHTS
2024–2025 · Europe

Offshore deportation centers and pushbacks: Europe debates the limits of human rights

In 2024 and 2025, several European countries began debating the creation of offshore deportation centers - facilities outside EU territory where asylum seekers would be processed, aiming to reduce migrant arrivals. Human rights organizations strongly criticized the measure, pointing out that it could circumvent fundamental legal protections. At the same time, cases of pushbacks - forced return of migrants before any analysis of their asylum claims - were documented in violation of international law.

CONNECTION TO BORDERS

Borders questions borders as power structures. The European debate shows that this power still decides - literally - who has the right to be heard.

CLIMATE
2024–2025 · Global

Climate migration: the next frontier - when the planet forces people from their homes

Alongside armed conflicts, climate change has become one of the leading causes of forced displacement. Extreme climate events - droughts, floods, hurricanes and rising sea levels - have already forced millions of people to abandon their homes in regions such as Bangladesh, the African Sahel, Pacific islands and parts of Central America. UN projections indicate that, without urgent climate action, up to 216 million people could become internal climate migrants by 2050.

CONNECTION TO BORDERS

M.I.A. asked who decides where people can live. With the climate crisis, the answer may be: the planet itself - and borders do not change that.
